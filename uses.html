    Forensic Science in Crime Investigation

    Forensic science plays a crucial role in solving crimes and bringing justice to victims. Here are some of the technologies and methods used in forensic science:

    Ballistics Analysis

    Ballistics analysis is the examination of firearms and ammunition to determine their source and the path of the bullet. This is useful in solving crimes involving guns, such as homicides and armed robberies. Techniques used in ballistics analysis include:

    • Comparison microscopy

      • This technique employs a comparison microscopeâ€”two separate compound or polarizing light microscopes connected via a bridge to a single viewing head, allowing an analyst to simultaneously observe two different samples under the same viewing conditions. Magnifications between 40X and 400X are typically used.

    • Bullet trajectory analysis

      • Bullet trajectory analysis is critical in regards to determining the path of a bullet and tracing its trajectory. The purpose is to determine approximately where the bullet(s) originated.

    Forensic Entomology

    Forensic entomology is the study of insects and other arthropods to determine the time of death, location of death, and other important information in criminal investigations. Techniques used in forensic entomology include:

    • Collection and identification of insect specimens

      • In order for insects to be properly identified and the best control recommendations to be made, the specimens must be in good condition and at least a minimum amount of collection information provided. Specimens that are incomplete, damaged, moldy, attached to tape, or squashed can only be identified to general groups. The better the condition of the specimen, the more precise identification and control recommendations.

    • Analysis of insect life cycles and behavior

      • The appearance of novel climates poses an important challenge for scientists trying to predict the ecological and evolutionary responses of organisms to climate change. To predict the geographic ranges of species, environmental niche models (ENMs) depend critically on how different seasonal and diurnal components of temperature and precipitation are correlated. Anticipated changes in this correlation structure during climate change, and the need to extrapolate to new environmental conditions, will limit the utility of ENMs for predicting changes in geographic range or abundance.
